Chapter 5: The Day the World Ended - AD 70 and the 'Coming' of Christ

"This generation will not pass away until all these things happen." - Matthew 24:34

In the previous chapter, we established that the troubling verses about eternal fire and judgment refer to the end of the Jewish age rather than eternity.

Now we need to understand how these prophecies were actually fulfilled.

The evidence is overwhelming: Jesus' "coming" and the "end of the age" happened exactly as He predicted, in AD 70.

Let's examine the historical evidence that proves these prophecies came to pass.

1. The Day of the Lord and the Coming on the Clouds

Whenever prophets announced a divine judgment, whether on Edom, Egypt or Babylon, they described cosmic fireworks.

Stars fell, suns darkened, moons refused to shine.

Take a look at the following judgments meted out to three different groups in the Old Testament. Try to imagine what your reaction would have been if you heard these words spoken over your nation or city.

A look at some DOOM-AND-GLOOM STUFF spoken to TWO nations and a city

EDOM (Isaiah 34:4-5)

"All the stars of the heavens will be dissolved and the sky rolled up like a scroll; all the starry host will fall like withered leaves from the vine, like shriveled figs from the fig tree. My sword has drunk its fill in the heavens; see, it descends in judgment on Edom."

EGYPT (Ezekiel 32:7-8)

"When I snuff you out, I will cover the heavens and darken their stars; I will cover the sun with a cloud, and the moon will not give its light. All the shining lights in the heavens I will darken over you; I will bring darkness over your land, declares the Sovereign LORD."

JERUSALEM (Zephaniah 1:14-18)

"The great day of the LORD is near—near and coming quickly. Listen! The cry on the day of the LORD will be bitter, the shouting of the warrior there. That day will be a day of wrath, a day of distress and anguish, a day of trouble and ruin, a day of darkness and gloom, a day of clouds and blackness, a day of trumpet and battle cry against the fortified cities and against the corner towers. I will bring distress on the people and they will walk like blind men, because they have sinned against the LORD. Their blood will be poured out like dust and their entrails like filth. Neither their silver nor their gold will be able to save them on the day of the LORD's wrath. In the fire of his jealousy the whole world will be consumed, for he will make a sudden end of all who live in the earth."

If you were around to hear this stuff spoken to you, my guess is you'd be afraid. Very afraid! You'd be certain that the world as you knew it would come to an end when these prophecies were fulfilled.

The interesting thing is that all three sets of prophecies were carried out! They have happened already. Past tense.

In the first case, the Babylonians defeated Edom, often referred to as Esau, from whom they descended, around the 6th century BCE. However, God takes the credit for Edom's defeat and destruction.

"But Esau I [God] have hated, and I [God] have turned his mountains into a wasteland." (Malachi 1:3)

Did God turn the mountains of Edom into a wasteland, or did the Babylonians? Clearly the Babylonian armies were God's instruments in this judgment.

In the case of Egypt, there's a curious additional piece of prophecy recorded in Scripture concerning its destruction.

"See, the LORD rides on a swift cloud and is coming to Egypt. The idols of Egypt tremble before him, and the hearts of the Egyptians melt within them." (Isaiah 19:1)

God is said to "ride on a cloud" and to "come" to Egypt. The next chapter in Isaiah records how the Assyrians destroyed Egypt (around 671-663 BCE). But we've just read how God is given the credit for Egypt's destruction. See the pattern?

Could the "cloud" be a reference to a swift, efficient, and effective army (in this case, the Assyrians) that God used to accomplish His purposes? God certainly didn't literally ride a physical cloud into Egypt.

It seems pretty clear that "coming on clouds" is symbolic language for divine judgment through earthly armies, rather than a literal astronomical event.

The Jerusalem prophecy mentioned above was fulfilled when it fell in the period 606 to 586 BC.

You will notice from the language used in all three prophecies that the world (our beloved earth) was not the object destroyed, and neither were all its inhabitants, even though that's what the prophecies appear to be saying. If you happened to be around when the drama of these judgments (the army invasions and destruction) were unfolding, you'd be forgiven for thinking that your world was falling apart.

Also no physical being was bobbing in the clouds shouting "Here I come! It's Judgment Day folks!"

Now, whiz forward in time to around 30 AD and look at what Jesus said:

"For the Son of Man is going to come in his Father's glory with his angels, and then he will reward each person according to what he has done. I tell you the truth, some who are standing here will not taste death before they see the Son of Man coming in his kingdom." (Matthew 16:27-28)

Sound familiar? How about this?

"Immediately after the distress of those days 'the sun will be darkened, and the moon will not give its light; the stars will fall from the sky, and the heavenly bodies will be shaken.' At that time the sign of the Son of Man will appear in the sky, and all the nations of the earth will mourn. They will see the Son of Man coming on the clouds of the sky, with power and great glory. And he will send his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will gather his elect from the four winds, from one end of the heavens to the other. I tell you the truth, this generation will certainly not pass away until all these things have happened." (Matthew 24:29-34)

Seems like déjà vu. This is quite similar to the Edom-Egypt-Jerusalem prophecies.

Before giving the above prophecy, Jesus predicted that the impressive temple in Jerusalem would be utterly destroyed. He went as far as to say, in reference to this temple...

"Not one stone...will be left on another." (Matthew 24:2)

I suspect His disciples clearly understood that this temple destruction would signify the end of an era of temple worship, so they asked Him when it would happen. They worded their question like this:

"Tell us," they said, "when will this happen, and what will be the sign of your coming and of the end of the age?" (Matthew 24:3)

Many today think the disciples were asking two separate questions, or that the answer Jesus gave was about two distinct events separated by a huge gap in time.

Well, Scripture interprets itself rather well, if you let it. The disciples were asking about Jesus' return to destroy the temple and bring about an end to the Jewish age. What's more, I'd venture that some had partial insight into His answer. This destruction happened in AD 70 when God, using the Romans as His "angels," destroyed the temple and wreaked havoc on Jerusalem.

So, am I saying that Jesus "came" in AD 70?

That's exactly what I'm saying! At the very least, that is one sense in which he came, based on letting the scriptures interpret themselves.

2. The Puzzling Scriptures That Make Perfect Sense

But read on. I haven't listed all the prophecies Jesus gave concerning the destruction of Jerusalem, but you will find it worthwhile to check out the events foretold. Some of these prophecies may have puzzled you before. I'll mention a few:

Puzzling Scripture No 1:

"[Jesus speaking] 'I tell you the truth, some who are standing here will not taste death before they see the Son of Man coming in his kingdom.'" (Matthew 16:28)

Jerusalem was invaded and the temple destroyed some thirty-five to forty years after Jesus spoke those words. In effect, many who were standing there listening to Jesus would have been alive when the destruction happened.

I'm always amused at those who seem to take delight in calling Jesus a failed prophet because they say this did not happen. All those who heard him say this died without seeing anything. I wouldn't be so quick to call Jesus a failed prophet, if I were you.

Puzzling Scripture No. 2:

Jesus was sending out His disciples and doing a really good motivational sell about how they'd be flogged and persecuted. He ended it by saying:

"When you are persecuted in one place, flee to another. I tell you the truth, you will not finish going through the cities of Israel before the Son of Man comes." (Matthew 10:23)

This makes no sense if it applies to an event that could happen in our lifetimes. I'm not planning to take a tour of the cities of Israel in the near future. But if I did, it's unlikely my trip would be interrupted by the Son of Man "coming" before I finished.

However, the prophecy does make sense because it is exactly what happened just prior to the events of AD 70.

Puzzling Scripture No. 3:

Here's something else Jesus said when He was dishing out signs to look out for at the time of his "coming." He calmly pointed out that at that time, people on their roofs would be well advised not to bother with stuff inside their houses. Here's what He said:

"Then let those who are in Judea flee to the mountains. Let no one on the roof of his house go down to take anything out of the house. Let no one in the field go back to get his cloak. How dreadful it will be in those days for pregnant women and nursing mothers! Pray that your flight will not take place in winter or on the Sabbath." (Matthew 24:16-20)

This clearly refers to an event from which fleeing would appear possible. If you lived in Judea, Jesus' advice was to head for the hills. Those on rooftops and in the fields would witness something that would give them the impression they might still have time to grab their possessions before fleeing. How can this refer to anything other than the events that unfolded in and around Jerusalem leading up to AD 70? Every reference to events like famines and earthquakes in Matthew chapter twenty-four happened before AD 70.

3. The Great Commission Already Fulfilled

I want to finish off with a clincher that comes from this same chapter in Matthew. Misinterpretation of this particular "sign" is responsible for much of the frantic missionary and "church planting" activity that happens today. Many refer to it as "the critical thing that must happen before Jesus can return to earth." You can probably hazard a guess at the "sign" to which I refer.

"And this gospel of the kingdom will be preached in the whole world as a testimony to all nations, and then the end will come." (Matthew 24:14)

I've often wondered why God would wait until at least one person from the last tribe on earth got to hear the gospel before deciding that the game's over. He could then sit back and smile since nobody's constitutional rights would have been violated because even the smallest of "minority groups" would have representation in heaven! Sounds a bit like "tokenism" in the name of political correctness!

According to Scripture, the gospel was indeed preached to all nations...before AD 70! Past tense. Done.

Ever heard this preached in church?

Multiple "comings" of Christ have occurred and will continue to occur throughout history, including the twenty-first century. They may not take the form and appearance you expect!

4. The Pattern of Divine Judgment

It was the vocabulary of upheaval, not astronomy.

As we saw earlier, Jesus borrowed that same language when He warned about Jerusalem's destruction.

"This generation will not pass away until all these things happen."

He meant exactly that generation.

And it happened.

When Rome marched in, the temple was reduced to rubble, and the world as they knew it ended.

To the disciples, that was the end of the world.

To history, it was the end of an age.

It's a little challenging to perform Temple rituals without a Temple.

Every "coming" of Christ since then follows the same pattern.

He arrives in moments of unveiling: in justice movements, awakenings, revolutions of conscience.

The clouds are always metaphorical.

They are the veils between ignorance and awareness.

5. The Real Meaning of Fire

The word "fire" is one of Scripture's most abused metaphors.

In Jesus' world, fire wasn't for punishment; it was for purification.

Gold was refined in fire, fields were cleared by it, bread was baked with it.

Fire transformed things ... it didn't annihilate them.

Paul, for all his confusion, glimpsed this when he wrote that everyone's work would be "tested by fire" and that the person whose work burns up "will still be saved, yet as through fire."

That's not damnation.

That's restoration by exposure.

Love is fire.

When it meets what is false, it burns.

When it meets what is true, it warms.

Same source, different effect.

6. What These Texts Really Reveal

If you listen closely, every so-called "hell verse" tells the same story.

Each one is a mirror, not a map.

Each one exposes the consequences of resisting love, not the mechanics of divine torture.

The flames are moral, not physical.

They burn away pride, ego, and the illusion of separation.

The "outer darkness" is the blindness of hearts that can't yet see love.

When translators turned metaphors into geography, they trapped God in their own fears.

They made heaven exclusive, hell eternal, and grace conditional.

But Jesus' language was always about awakening, not punishment.

7. How Fear Survived the Translation

Why did the church cling to these mistranslations for so long?

Because fear works.

It fills pews, sells books, and keeps donations steady.

If people believe they're one heartbeat away from eternal flames, you can steer their lives like a remote-controlled car.

Fear is the oldest form of social engineering, and religion perfected it.

But truth has a stubborn habit of leaking through even the tightest doctrine.

Every generation produces souls who ask, "What if love really never fails?"

And that question is the crack in hell's foundation.

But What About the "Strongest" Verses?

You might be thinking, "That's all well and good, but what about the verses that seem most clearly to support eternal punishment?"

Let me address two of the most frequently cited passages that appear to contradict this message of universal reconciliation.

The Lake That Blazes with Fire and Brimstone

Perhaps no verse sounds more terrifying than this one from Revelation:

"But as for the cowardly...the unbelieving...murderers...and all liars, their portion will be in the lake that burns with fire and sulfur." - Revelation 21:8

This passage troubles even the most confident universalists. But let's pause and ask what it's actually describing.

Notice who ends up in this so-called "lake of fire": everyone.

Have you ever told a lie? Felt afraid? Had doubts? According to this verse, we all qualify for the "lake that blazes with fire and brimstone."

But here's what transforms this from a threat into a promise of healing:

The Greek word for "brimstone" is theion—which comes from theos, meaning "divine."

In ancient times, sulfur (brimstone) was known for its purifying properties. It was used as divine incense, to ward off disease, and to cleanse what was impure.

The "lake of fire" isn't punitive: it's medicinal.

It's the process by which love burns away everything that isn't love until only the real you remains.

Those difficult seasons you've walked through ... the times when old patterns died, relationships ended, illusions shattered ... those were your personal encounters with the "lake of fire."

And they saved you.

This same imagery appears in Revelation's vision of the final judgment:

"The devil...was thrown into the lake of burning sulfur...and will be tormented day and night for ever and ever...The sea gave up the dead that were in it, and death and Hades gave up the dead that were in them, and each person was judged according to what they had done...Anyone whose name was not found written in the book of life was thrown into the lake of fire." - Revelation 20:10-15

But remember: We've already seen how "forever" can be mistranslated from aionios.

And notice the beautiful detail: even in this final scene, "death and Hades gave up the dead".

They don't keep them. They surrender them.

Even death itself becomes a servant of life in the end.

The Wages of Sin

Another verse that seems to contradict universal hope is Paul's statement:

"The wages of sin is death, but the gift of God is eternal life." - Romans 6:23

This sounds clear: sin leads to death, not eternal life.

But notice what Paul doesn't say.

He doesn't say, "The wages of sin is eternal death."

He doesn't say, "The wages of sin is eternal torment."

He simply says, "death."

And what is death, in the biblical sense?

Separation from life: from the awareness of love that sustains everything.

But here's the beautiful truth hidden in Paul's own words: death is not the end of the story.

Even Paul, in all his complexity, understood that God's plan was bigger than our failures:

"Just as one trespass resulted in condemnation for all people, so also one righteous act resulted in justification and life for all people." - Romans 5:18

"For all people."

Not "for some people."

For all people.

Paul's theology contains both paradox and promise. He speaks of wages earned and gifts given, of condemnation for all and justification for all. Though it is not in the scope of this book to address, the "condemnation for all" is a doctrine Paul supported which does not line up with the rest of scripture. We are not "born sinful" because of Adam and Eve. Our Father does not hold anyone accountable for the sins of their parents, the sins of Adam and Eve, or anyone in between, according to numerous scriptures including Ezekiel 18:20:

"The soul who sins shall die. The son shall NOT bear the guilt of the father, nor the father bear the guilt of the son. The righteousness of the righteous shall be upon himself, and the wickedness of the wicked shall be upon himself." (Ezekiel 18:20)

But even if we were 'born sinful', what Paul is saying is that one act of righteousness (Jesus death) restores life for all. Everyone. No exceptions.

The key is understanding that death is not the destination: it's the doorway.

Every ending is also a beginning. Every death leads to resurrection. Every descent prepares an ascent.

That's not wishful thinking. That's the pattern of love itself.

Love never gives up: not on you, not on anyone, not on creation itself.

Summary

The verses that once chained us were never God's threats.

They were warnings against blindness, parables about compassion, poetry about purification.

They were Jesus saying, Wake up before the world you built collapses.

This chapter has demonstrated that those scary-sounding words about "stars falling from the sky," "coming in the clouds in judgment," and other apocalyptic destruction terms were used metaphorically for specific historical events, including the destruction of the temple (and the associated sacrificial system) in AD 70. We've taken those words and darkened them with literal meaning, trapping ourselves in fear of an endless future punishment that was never intended.

The historical evidence is clear: Jesus' prophecies about the end of the age were fulfilled in AD 70, exactly when He predicted. The temple fell, the Jewish age ended, and a new era of grace began, initiated by the RADICALLY DIFFERENT GOD Jesus revealed when he called him 'Father.'
